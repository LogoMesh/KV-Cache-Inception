## **Part 1: Characterize the EMNLP 2026 Zeitgeist**

To successfully pivot the current manuscript into a highly competitive submission for the Conference on Empirical Methods in Natural Language Processing (EMNLP) 2026, it is imperative to deeply characterize the current editorial zeitgeist of the venue.1 A rigorous analysis of EMNLP 2024 and 2025 acceptance trends, combined with the stated priorities for the 2026 program, reveals a clear preference for methodologies that illuminate underlying model mechanics or provide robust, scalable infrastructure for deployed systems, particularly those that engage with the "New Missions for NLP Research" theme.1

An analysis of heavily accepted areas in the mechanistic interpretability and activation steering domains reveals three dominant vectors. The first heavily accepted area focuses on Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) and feature disentanglement. The EMNLP program committee demonstrates a high appetite for SAE-driven analysis that decomposes polysemantic neurons into monosemantic, interpretable features, as evidenced by acceptances such as "A Survey on Sparse Autoencoders: Interpreting the Internal Mechanisms of Large Language Models" in Findings of EMNLP 2025 2 and "Route Sparse Autoencoder to Interpret Large Language Models" in the EMNLP 2025 main track.4 The second area of intense interest revolves around multi-turn dialogue safety and automated red-teaming. As large language models increasingly operate within agentic, multi-turn workflows, the safety community has pivoted toward vulnerabilities that only manifest over extended interactions. Papers like "MUSE: MCTS-Driven Red Teaming Framework for Enhanced Multi-Turn Dialogue Safety in Large Language Models" 5 explicitly prove that EMNLP values Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) highly when applied to dialogue trajectory generation and safety alignment. The third dominant area is multimodal and vision-language activation steering. The representation engineering (RepE) paradigm has largely migrated from text-only models to multimodal architectures. Works focusing on hallucination mitigation via representation engineering in Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), such as "SteerVLM: Robust Model Control through Lightweight Activation Steering for Vision Language Models" 7 and "SHARP: Steering Hallucination in LVLMs via Representation Engineering" 9, currently saturate the hallucination-mitigation tracks.

Conversely, several areas remain critically under-served, presenting high-yield opportunities for a methodologically sound contribution. The most prominent under-served area is inference-time compute scaling operating directly in continuous latent space, often referred to as "System 2" reasoning.11 While discrete test-time scaling methods, such as Best-of-N sampling and text-space MCTS, are well-established, applying search algorithms directly to continuous state-space exploration is a nascent subfield.15 The literature is just beginning to conceptualize continuous chains of thought without discrete tokenization bottlenecks, as seen in recent exploratory works.17 A methodologically rigorous continuous tree search algorithm would effectively define this emerging space. Another significantly under-served area involves the interpretability of the KV-cache as a cognitive "working memory" mechanism.19 The current NLP community predominantly treats the KV-cache as a systems-engineering bottleneck requiring mathematical compression.3 The cognitive role of the KV-cache in maintaining long-context fidelity and its temporal degradation dynamics remain largely unexplored from a mechanistic standpoint.24 Finally, the activation-level signatures of multi-turn adversarial attacks represent an under-served niche. While behavioral evaluations of multi-turn jailbreaks are common, analyzing the "adversarial restlessness" or the shifting trajectory of latent states across progressive conversation turns is exceedingly rare, offering a pristine avenue for novel telemetry applications.25

In contrast, one specific area must be deemed over-served and should be aggressively avoided: static activation steering applied to single-turn text hallucination mitigation. The foundational premise of extracting a linear probe and projecting it as a static corrective vector during single-turn generation has been exhaustively explored.26 Reviewers in 2026 expect dynamic, adaptive, or deeply context-aware interventions.28 Framing an advanced MCTS infrastructure merely as a novel technique to incrementally reduce single-turn textual hallucination 10 will almost certainly result in rejection due to insufficient novelty relative to the established baseline.10

Navigating these trends requires a firm grasp of the unstated EMNLP reviewer norms, which differ significantly from those at venues like ICLR or NeurIPS.31 A NeurIPS reviewer might champion a paper primarily for its algorithmic elegance, such as mathematically proving an ![][image1] memory overhead for tree search. An EMNLP reviewer, however, applies a strict mandate for empirical NLP grounding.33 The infrastructure must demonstrably resolve a specific NLP phenomenon, reveal something fundamental about language representations, or enable new behavioral discoveries. Furthermore, the EMNLP 2026 "New Missions for NLP Research" theme explicitly calls for work that treats LLMs as research instruments.1 Infrastructure papers are expected to demonstrate how they enable the community to interrogate questions that were previously computationally intractable. Finally, EMNLP reviewers aggressively penalize claims of deep "understanding" or "alignment" if the empirical evidence only supports a localized geometric shift in vector space 34; methodological claims must be strictly scoped to the empirical evidence presented.

**Title:** Test-Time Scaling in Continuous Latent Space: Reversible KV-Cache MCTS for System-2 Reasoning

**Abstract:**

Recent advancements in large language models have demonstrated that allocating additional compute during inference—often termed "System 2" reasoning—significantly enhances performance on complex logical tasks. However, existing test-time scaling methods, such as Best-of-N sampling and discrete Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), operate entirely in the combinatorial space of text tokens. These methods suffer from an exponential memory blowup (![][image5]) caused by the necessity of parallel cache duplication. We introduce a paradigm shift: performing test-time search directly in the continuous latent space of the model's Key-Value (KV) cache. We present **Reversible KV-Cache MCTS**, an inference-time algorithm that mutates a frozen Transformer's representations via in-place arithmetic and rolls back to exact baseline states at each tree expansion. We prove that an FP32 accumulator guarantees zero-drift reversibility (Theorem 1), achieving a memory bound independent of the branching factor. Guided by a dual-channel telemetry matrix that evaluates the representational *certainty* and *hallucination-stress* of intermediate latent states, our search algorithm systematically navigates the continuous reasoning manifold. Evaluated on Llama 3.2 1B and 3B models, our framework establishes the computational viability of gradient-free, memory-bounded continuous reasoning, achieving higher latent signal quality per compute unit than unstructured generation.

**Contributions:**

1. **Reversible Monte Carlo Tree Search in KV-Cache Space:** We introduce the first gradient-free, memory-bounded inference-time search algorithm that operates directly in the continuous latent space of a frozen LLM, effectively reducing the memory overhead of tree search from ![][image5] to a constant factor of the base cache.  
2. **Latent Allocation of Test-Time Compute (System 2):** We formalize a novel paradigm for System-2 reasoning where computational budgets are spent exploring and optimizing internal representations via heuristic tree search, rather than autoregressively generating and discarding discrete text tokens.  
3. **Telemetry-Guided Latent Optimization:** We deploy a per-layer telemetry matrix that fuses Representation Engineering (RepE) certainty projections with H-Neuron stress signals, providing a differentiable, non-textual reward function that effectively guides the MCTS to high-confidence latent reasoning states at the 1B and 3B parameter scales.

**Problem Formulation (Revised Framing):**

Standard test-time compute scaling relies on the autoregressive generation of discrete reasoning traces, commonly known as Chain-of-Thought. This discrete text-space bottleneck restricts exploration: models must commit to rigid token realizations, and search algorithms must duplicate massive KV-caches to evaluate alternative branches. We formulate "System 2" reasoning not as the generation of additional text, but as an optimization problem over the continuous representational manifold ![][image6] of the model's hidden states. Let ![][image7] be a frozen language model. Under controlled, reversible perturbations of the cached KV tensors, we define a continuous search objective: maximize the model's internal representational certainty (measured via a linear certainty monitor ![][image8]) while minimizing structural hallucination stress (measured via H-Neuron activation ![][image2]). The central computational problem is how to systematically explore this manifold ![][image9] with deep branching factors without incurring the ![][image10] memory invalidation inherent to standard Transformer architectures.

## **Part 8: One Concrete Wildcard Recommendation**

**The Wildcard:** Shift the intervention target from the deepest layers of the residual stream to the earliest layers, and reframe the algorithm around "KV-Cache Working Memory Consolidation."

This conservative structural reconceptualization completely sidesteps the saturated "activation steering for output generation" market. Recent work at EMNLP and ACL has heavily highlighted the "Contextual Memory" aspect of the KV-cache, noting that LLMs suffer from "context rot" where stale associations degrade retrieval accuracy.21 Instead of using Reversible MCTS to steer generation, this framing deploys it on the *prefill phase* of a long-context document. The MCTS tests minor latent perturbations to the KV representations of older tokens. The reward function optimizes for the retention of critical semantic information—measured by top-layer RepE probes—while compressing or "sleeping" irrelevant tokens. This approach taps directly into the highly sought-after long-context interpretability and KV-cache memory management tracks.19 It utilizes the exact same mathematics, the exact same FP32 rollback, and the exact same hardware envelope, but tells a wildly novel story: using continuous search to simulate sleep-dependent memory consolidation and selective forgetting in frozen LLMs.
