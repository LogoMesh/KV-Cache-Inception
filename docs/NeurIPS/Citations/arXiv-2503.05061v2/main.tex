\begin{abstract}

Reliable evaluation of large language models (LLMs) is critical as their deployment rapidly expands, particularly in high-stakes domains. The LLM-as-a-Judge framework, which uses prompted LLMs to evaluate response quality, is appealing due to its scalability, low cost, and strong correlations with human stylistic preferences. However, it remains unclear how accurately these methods can assess response quality when correctness matters more than style. To address this gap, we introduce the Business and Finance Fundamentals Benchmark (\evalbenchmark), a dataset of 160 challenging questions and long-form responses authored by financial professionals. These experts subsequently evaluated the correctness of 1,200 responses generated by a diverse set of LLMs on both \evalbenchmark{} and a challenging subset of MT-Bench. With this expert-annotated dataset of judgments (\judgebenchmark{}), we analyze the agreement between a suite of automated grading methods and human experts. While we observe that LLM Judges are more reliable than other grading methods, our findings reveal a clear pattern in LLM Judge performance: when not provided with a correct reference, judges show high agreement with human experts \emph{only on questions the judges were able to correctly answer themselves}. We demonstrate that providing the judges with expert-written references largely mitigates this issue, highlighting the limits of using LLM-as-a-Judge without any form of human verification.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

For text generation tasks such as translation, summarization, and question answering, models provide free-form responses that are evaluated for some notion of overall quality \citep{Celikyilmaz2020EvaluationOT}. Because of the open-ended nature of the output space, automated evaluation methods often rely on lexical overlap heuristics that compare model outputs to trusted references (e.g., BLEU, \citealp{bleu}; ROUGE, \citealp{rouge}; and METEOR, \citealp{meteor}) or---more recently---on methods such as reward models \citep{ouyang2022training,kocmi-federmann-2023-large,fu-etal-2024-gptscore} and LLM-as-a-Judge \citep{mtbench,JudgeSurvey}. Using LLM-as-a-Judge for evaluation has become increasingly prominent due to its flexibility and high correlation with human preference. Benchmarks such as MT-Bench \citep{mtbench}, AlpacaEval2.0 \citep{alpacaeval2}, ArenaHardAuto \citep{arenahard}, and WildBench \citep{lin2024wildbench} are commonly used to evaluate the capabilities of frontier language models.

\input{figures/figure1}

The LLM-as-a-Judge framework is typically used in the following way: a model that we want to evaluate (the \textbf{candidate model}) generates responses to a set of benchmark questions. Then, to evaluate these responses, an LLM (the \textbf{judge model} or \textbf{LLM Judge}) is prompted to produce some kind of quality score from each response. The candidate model's response can be graded in isolation (\textbf{single grading}) or in comparison to an additional candidate model's response (\textbf{pairwise grading}). Each of the aforementioned benchmarks validates their use of LLM-as-a-Judge by measuring correlations with human preference annotations. Human preferences can naturally vary along many different axes, making it difficult to determine exactly why one response might be preferred to another \citep{DBLP:conf/icml/SorensenMFGMRYJ24,SOSBench,Szymanski2024ComparingCD}.

In some high-stakes domains such as business and finance, however, correctness outweighs other attributes \citep{bommasani2021opportunities,SOSBench,bizbench}. In these settings, accurately judging response quality hinges on identifying whether a response is correct, which itself can require complex reasoning and domain knowledge \citep{JudgeBench,mtbench}. In this work, we investigate the following question: \emph{Can LLMs make reliable judgments of correctness, and therefore quality, in these high-stakes domains?}


Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We create {\bf \evalbenchmark{}: a new, challenging conversational benchmark with a focus on correctness } This business and finance themed benchmark contains 80 conversations spanning 160 questions with long-form reference answers created and verified by domain experts.
\item Using data from \evalbenchmark{} and MT-Bench, we create the \textbf{V}erified \textbf{E}xpert \textbf{R}esponse \textbf{D}ataset for \textbf{I}dentifying \textbf{C}orrectness over \textbf{T}ext \textbf{S}tyle, or {\bf \judgebenchmark{}: a dataset of expert judgments of correctness for 1,200 LLM responses} to evaluate the reliability of methods of pairwise and single grading.
\item We demonstrate that on \judgebenchmark{}, {\bf whether an LLM Judge agrees with human experts is strongly related to its ability to answer the underlying benchmark question and the correctness of the provided reference} (\cref{fig:one}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

To use as little synthetic data as possible in constructing our core benchmarks, we worked with human experts to create challenging questions with reference solutions, and annotate response correctness. A more detailed description of our annotators and annotation process can be found in \cref{sec:annotator_details}. We introduce a novel dataset (\evalbenchmark{}), a corrected subset of an existing dataset (\cevalbenchmark{}), and a set of human expert labels of model response correctness over these two datasets (\judgebenchmark{}).\footnote{All of these datasets are released with an Apache 2.0 license.}


\subsection{\evalbenchmark{}}

Our goal was to collect a benchmark that was conversational in nature but with unambiguous correctness. To have the same expertise available for both question creation and grading, we recruited a team of experts within a single high-stakes domain: business and finance. Although this limited the diversity of the underlying dataset, this tradeoff was necessary to scale both dataset creation and response grading.

We collected 80 two-turn conversational questions with gold reference answers, all written entirely from scratch by domain-experts. These questions are designed to be challenging and unambiguous, integrating both business and financial domain knowledge with quantitative reasoning. A sample question and reference answer are shown in \cref{fig:bffbench-sample}.

The question-response pairs were developed by a team of financial professionals. Each pair underwent an iterative quality control process and verified.
Questions were designed to have unambiguous answers to facilitate unambiguous correctness judgments.
Many require mathematical manipulation, from simple arithmetic to the application of financial formulas, whereas others test domain knowledge. Given their intended difficulty and technical scope, each question-response pair required approximately one hour to create, clean, and verify.

\subsection{\cevalbenchmark{}}


To increase the diversity of our judgment set, we collected data from the math and reasoning subsets (40 total questions) from MT-Bench. This subset, unlike MT-Bench's more open-ended categories,\footnote{For example, MT-Bench includes examples such as: ``Compose an engaging travel blog post about a recent trip to Hawaii, highlighting cultural experiences and must-see attractions.'' which admit multiple valid responses.} is evaluated with references by default in the MT-Bench pipeline and leads to responses that tend to be unambiguously correct or incorrect.

Inspired by \citet{inflectionai}, we reviewed the provided GPT-4-generated reference answers for these 40 questions and \emph{found that 15 (37.5\%) were either incorrect or inconsistent}.\footnote{By ``inconsistent'', we mean that the final answer is correct but the reasoning is invalid.} An example of one of these errors in provided in \cref{fig:mt-bench-wrong} in \cref{sec:cmt_examples}. We created correct references for each question by manually re-writing and verifying the existing references. These corrected references include the correct answer and a coherent reasoning chain, detailing all necessary sub-steps. We refer to this corrected subset as \cevalbenchmark{}. %

\subsection{\judgebenchmark{}}
We collected responses on \cevalbenchmark{} and \evalbenchmark{} from a diverse set of six models across various model families and sizes: \texttt{Gemma 2 2b} \citep{gemma2}, \texttt{Qwen 2.5 7b} \citep{qwen2.5}, \texttt{Phi-4} \citep{phi4}, \texttt{Yi 1.5 34B} \citep{yi15}, \texttt{Llama 3.3 70B} \citep{llama3}, and \texttt{GPT-4o} \citep{gpt4o}.\footnote{Our analysis entails domain-expert human annotations per response; extending to additional models requires new annotation rounds, so we prioritize diversity of model size/family over recency.} Each model was evaluated with a temperature of 0.7 using vLLM \citep{kwon2023efficient}, resulting in a total of 1,200 question-response pairs.\footnote{We used 8 80GB A100 GPUs for inference.}

For each question-response pair, we collected three annotations of correctness. For questions from \evalbenchmark{}, the same financial professionals served as annotators. Annotators received the human-written reference solution and could use external resources except LLM-based chatbots. They were instructed to grade strictly: if any part was wrong, the entire response was marked wrong. Full guidelines are in \cref{sec:annotatorguidelines}. Dataset statistics are in \cref{tab:combined_dataset}.

\input{tables/datasets}

Annotators were instructed to select from ``Incorrect'', ``Correct'', and ``Not Sure.'' 54\% of the question-response pairs received unanimously consistent annotations, i.e., all three annotations were ``Incorrect'' or ``Correct''. 86\% of the questions had at most 1 annotation that differed from the other 2 annotations (e.g., 2 annotations were ``Incorrect'' and 1 was ``Not Sure''). 14\% of the questions had either no consensus or a consensus of ``Not Sure''. We deem these 14\% of examples to be ambiguous and exclude them from our experiments. %



\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

We experiment on two judgment tasks derived from our \judgebenchmark{} dataset, measuring agreement with human annotators for both the standard LLM-as-a-Judge pipeline and a collection of baselines. The final dataset composition is in \cref{tab:combined_dataset}.


\subsection{Judgment Tasks}



For the {\bf single grading} task, the judge is given a question-response pair and must classify it as ``Correct'' or ``Incorrect''.  For the {\bf pairwise grading} task, the judge is given a question and two responses and must determine which is correct. We ensure that each pair has one response labeled ``Correct'' and one labeled ``Incorrect'' by the annotators. Despite not reflecting how judges are used in practice, we do this to provide a performance metric that can be fairly compared to the single grading task.

We evaluate both tasks using Cohen's $\kappa$ \citep{CohenKappa}; an alternative version of the results using F1 are in \cref{tab:right-wrong-table-f1-v1}. More details on metrics and confidence intervals are in \cref{sec:confidenceintervals}.



  


\subsection{LLM-as-a-Judge}

Our judges consist of the same set of six models used as candidates. The only exception is \texttt{Gemma 2 2B}, which was used only as a candidate.\footnote{\texttt{Gemma 2 2B} was too inconsistent in its ability to produce the required judgment output format.} The judge model is allowed to use Chain-Of-Thought \citep{ChainOfThought} to first generate a set of reasoning steps before generating a final answer.  To reduce the overall variance in this analysis, we compute our judgments using self-consistency \citep{selfconsistency}, taking the majority vote over five separate inferences generated with a temperature of 0.7.

For the single grading case, the model is prompted to grade only the correctness of the model response. We include the full interaction history for the multi-turn questions within the judgment prompt. The model receives a similar prompt for the pairwise grading case, but with instructions to select the more correct response of the two provided. Following \citet{mtbench}, we run each pair twice, reversing the ordering of the references so as to account for any positional bias the judge model may have (e.g., always favoring the first model's response). If the judge model's selection differs between the two runs, we count it as a tie.

We use a version of the MT-Bench prompts modified to focus on correctness, which can be found in \cref{sec:judgeprompts}. To ensure our results are robust to the underlying prompt, we re-run our single grading experiments with a separate prompt that contains a rephrasing of the same instructions. These results are discussed in \cref{sec:prompt_sensitivity}, but we find no meaningful variation.

\subsection{Grading References}

Our main analysis concerns the impact of the provided reference on the judge's performance. Specifically, we look at three main types of provided references: (1) \textbf{\textit{None}}: no reference is provided within the LLM Judge's prompt, (2) \textbf{\textit{Self}}: the LLM Judge provides its own reference answer and (3) \textbf{\textit{Human}}: a human provides a manually created reference answer. In the \textit{None} case, the model is prompted to directly evaluate the provided response without a reference. For all other cases the prompt is identical except for the text of the reference.


In addition, we evaluate three types of baseline reference. \textit{Wrong} refers to human-edited versions of the \textit{Human} references that try to maintain the overall style but are incorrect. Each has some amount of inaccurate information or incorrect reasoning that changes the final answer to the question. Any correct response would then be incorrect with respect to these references. However, it would not impact the grading of responses that were already incorrect unless they happened to make the same error. \textit{Random} are randomly selected references from other questions. These references are non-informative without being actively misleading.

\subsection{Baselines}


We evaluate three automated grading baseline methods of response quality: lexical overlap metrics, embedding models, and reward models. Because these methods output quality scores, we focus our baseline evaluation on pairwise grading, labeling the response with the higher score as correct. The lexical overlap metrics capture how well-designed heuristics can perform on this task. We score each potential response with respect to the gold human-written reference. We evaluate BLEU \citep{bleu}, ROUGE \citep{rouge}, and METEOR \citep{meteor}. We convert an embedding model into a quality score by computing the cosine similarity between each response and the human-written reference. We select five high-performing, open-weight embedding models on the English split of MTEB \citep{mteb}.\footnote{MTEB does not require a public release of the model weights, so we were unable to access some of the top five models.} Reward models are LLM-based models explicitly trained to predict human preferences and are increasingly used within alignment pipelines. We compute the reward for each response directly, and select the response with the highest reward as being the ``Correct'' response. We select the five highest performing reward models on RewardBench \citep{RewardBench}. 
 
The full list of the baselines evaluated and their performance can be found in \cref{sec:baselines}.

The results between the single and pairwise grading do not directly reflect whether one setup is better than the other. The pairwise dataset was created to always have one correct and one incorrect response from two different candidate models. The large differences in response quality can make the judge model's grading relatively straightforward. As expected, we observed higher agreement with the human annotators in the pairwise grading setup than in the single grading setup.

\section{Analysis}

\paragraph{LLM-as-a-Judge with human-written references leads to the highest agreement with the human annotators.} Overall, providing a human-written reference improves judges' agreement with human annotators for all evaluated judges across both the single and pairwise cases. Notably, providing a human reference to a relatively small model (such as \texttt{Qwen 2.5 7B}) can yield better judgments than using a larger model without human references (such as \texttt{GPT-4o}) on the same set of responses.

\paragraph{Without a human-written reference, LLM Judges only achieve high agreement on questions they were able to answer as candidates.} For each question in the evaluation, we have a label indicating whether the judge model was able to answer that question correctly when used as a candidate model.\footnote{The label is the consensus judgment of the expert annotators.}  When we condition the judge's agreement on this label in \Cref{tab:right-wrong-table}, there are clear differences in judgment performance. For questions the judge model was unable to correctly answer, there are large differences in the agreement between using different reference types. This is especially the case for higher-performing judges. For example, when \texttt{GPT-4o} uses its own responses to questions as a reference (i.e., \textit{Self} response), the Cohen's $\kappa$ decreases from 0.86 to 0.16 in the pairwise case on questions \texttt{GPT-4o} could not answer. On questions the judge had answered correctly, providing human references still improves performance. However, while these differences are meaningful, they are much lower than when the model was incorrect. This is largely due to the higher judgment performance on correct instances.

\input{tables/correct_incorrect_table}










\paragraph{Different types of reference lead to different profiles of judge behavior.} One common judgment bias is self-preference \citep{panickssery2024llmevaluatorsrecognizefavor}, in which a model tends to overrate its own responses. To quantify this bias, we analyzed the different types of errors the LLM Judges were making. In this context, the false positive rate (FPR) represents the rate at which the model erroneously labels incorrect responses as being correct and the false negative rate (FNR) represents the rate at which the model erroneously labels correct responses as incorrect. A model with a strong self-preference bias would exhibit a higher FPR and a lower FNR when grading its own responses compared to when grading responses from other models.



\cref{fig:affinity_bias} displays the FPR and FNR aggregated over each judge with the \textit{Wrong}, \textit{Random}, \textit{None}, \textit{Self}, and \textit{Human} reference types on the single grading task.\footnote{Metrics per judge can be found in \cref{fig:affinity_per_judge_all}.} For every reference type, we see that on average models have a higher FPR when grading their own responses. The gap is particularly large when the model is provided with its own generated reference or no reference at all. Providing a human reference reduces both the overall rate of both error types and the relative difference between a model's judgment of its own responses and those of others.



When the model is given a \textit{Wrong} reference, the FPR is low and the FNR is high, which suggests that the incorrect information contained in the reference leads judges to erroneously classify correct responses as incorrect.\footnote{Here, we mean erroneously in terms of the actual correctness of the underlying response. When given a \textit{Wrong} reference, the correct model behavior would be to rule everything incorrect, unless it happened to make the same error contained in the \textit{Wrong} reference} However, this effect is not observed with the \textit{Random} reference. This evidence suggests that judges are sensitive to the correctness of the reference, and providing a slightly incorrect reference can in some cases be worse than providing a completely unrelated reference or no reference at all. 
\input{figures/affinity_bias}


Even when provided with a \textit{Wrong} reference that should make every other response incorrect, LLM Judges were still judging responses to be correct. We analyzed these judgments to understand why this occurred. Although most were simply reasoning errors (e.g. claiming the reference and the response agreed when they did not), we observed cases of a judge model identifying the reference as being incorrect and ``overruling'' the reference to correctly grade the response. An example of this ``overruling'' behavior is provided in \cref{sec:judge-refusal}.



\paragraph{Reference correctness matters more than being human-written.}
To better understand what makes human-written references lead to higher agreement with our human annotators, we compared performance across human-written gold references, a set of \texttt{GPT-4o} references judged by humans to be correct, a set of \textit{Wrong} references, and a set of \textit{Random} references. \cref{tab:good-ref} presents results for these  reference types across all five judges. The disparity observed in the previous section between human-written and synthetic references diminishes. There is also a significant gap between the \textit{Human} references and the \textit{Wrong} references. This suggests that the most important part of human-written references is their correctness, rather than any other superficial or stylistic attributes.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:limitations}

{\bf We release \evalbenchmark{} and \judgebenchmark{}, two new datasets aimed at evaluating how well LLM-as-a-Judge grades response correctness in high-stakes domains.} These datasets enable the study of model performance both as candidates and as judges on difficult questions that require deep business and finance domain knowledge.

{\bf Using LLM-as-a-Judge does not alleviate the need for human oversight.} Our results demonstrate that LLM-as-a-Judge can lead to high agreement when: the LLM Judge  (1) can already answer the underlying question or (2) is provided with a correct reference. Having confidence in a judge model requires meeting at least one of these two conditions.

{\bf LLM-as-a-Judge performance is sensitive to the provided reference.} We observe large variations in performance when changing only the provided reference in the judgment prompt. Providing accurate references increases agreement with human annotators, while providing slightly incorrect references can be worse than providing no reference at all. The disparity in agreement is most pronounced for benchmark questions the judge model answers incorrectly. Improving agreement on such questions is key to advancing the reliability of evaluations of frontier models when using the LLM-as-a-Judge framework.

Therefore, {\bf we strongly recommend practitioners (at minimum) verify their reference responses.} The results in \cref{tab:good-ref} indicate that verifying responses generated by a model rather than manually writing and checking answers via annotators is sufficient. Although this verification process still requires human effort, our results suggest that this is unavoidable for developing trustworthy LLM-as-a-Judge pipelines. However, note that although using verified references may improve the overall agreement metrics, it may come at the cost of increased bias. Specifically, using a model's own references exacerbates the self-preference bias compared to providing no reference at all. We leave a more in-depth analysis of the potential pitfalls of using verified, synthetic references to future work.
