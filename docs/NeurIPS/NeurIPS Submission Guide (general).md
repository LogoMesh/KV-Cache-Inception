### **Architecture and Typographical Rigor of the Submission Document**

The compilation of the submission artifact requires strict adherence to a single PDF structure, which is hard-capped at a 50MB file size limit.5 The document must be compiled using the official 2026 LaTeX style file (neurips\_2026.sty) specifically rewritten for LaTeX2e.5 Legacy formats, including Microsoft Word, RTF, and earlier LaTeX 2.09 iterations, have been wholly deprecated and are no longer supported.5

The typographical constraints are unyielding. The text must be strictly confined within a rectangular bounding box measuring 5.5 inches (33 picas) wide and 9 inches (54 picas) long, situated on standard US Letter paper sizing.13 The left margin must be fixed at exactly 1.5 inches (9 picas).15 Submissions that attempt to violate the NeurIPS style—such as by artificially decreasing margins, altering line spacing, or reducing font sizes to circumvent page limits—will be identified by automated parsing scripts and subsequently desk rejected without consideration of their scientific merits.5 Authors must ensure their final PDF compilation contains only Type 1 or Embedded TrueType fonts, as missing font rendering remains a primary cause of visual failure during the review process.13 Display mathematics must utilize proper LaTeX commands for unnumbered display math (e.g., \\\[... \\\]) rather than bare TeX commands to ensure that line numbering is generated correctly alongside the equations during the double-blind review phase.14

The internal architecture of the single PDF must follow a precise sequence 5:

The **Main Content** is strictly limited to nine pages, encapsulating the introduction, related work, methodologies, empirical results, conclusions, figures, and tables.5 If the manuscript is accepted, the authors will be granted an additional tenth content page for the final camera-ready version to integrate feedback received during the review and rebuttal phases.5

The **References** section immediately follows the main content and is excluded from the nine-page limit.5 The NeurIPS format defaults to the natbib package; authors may use either author/year or numeric citation formats, provided absolute internal consistency is maintained throughout the manuscript.13

The **Technical Appendices** follow the references and are similarly excluded from the nine-page limit.5 A critical philosophical distinction must be understood regarding the appendix: it serves exclusively as optional supplementary reading for the reviewing committee.13 The main nine-page text must stand entirely independently. Attempting to offload critical experiments, core architectural details, or foundational mathematical proofs required to validate the central claims of the paper into the appendix is considered highly inappropriate and frequently results in rejection, as reviewers are under no obligation to read the supplementary pages.13

Finally, the **NeurIPS Paper Checklist** must be appended to the very end of the single PDF document, following the references and any optional appendices.5 The checklist is inherently integrated into the LaTeX style file, and papers submitted without it will face immediate desk rejection.17

### **Supplementary Artifacts: Code, Data, and Double-Blind Confidentiality**

Separate from the single PDF submission, authors are strongly encouraged to upload supplementary materials, such as localized datasets, training code, or multimedia demonstrations (e.g., videos), within a single ZIP archive capped at 100MB.5 The overarching policy dictates that if any of the main contributions of the paper depends upon an experimental result, it is the best practice for responsible research to include the code that directly produces this result.18

The code submission should ideally be entirely self-contained and executable by a third party.18 It must include both training and evaluation scripts, along with an explicit specification of environment dependencies.18 If the code cannot be made self-contained or executable, the authors are mandated to provide a detailed explanation. Acceptable reasons for non-executable code submissions typically include reliance on specialized, proprietary hardware accelerators, custom robotic platforms, or dependencies on non-open-sourced commercial libraries (such as paid mathematical programming solvers or commercial simulators) that do not constitute the actual scientific algorithmic contribution of the paper.5

Confidentiality and anonymity are paramount during the supplementary upload process. At the time of submission, all code, directory structures, internal comments, and localized data must be meticulously anonymized to comply with the double-blind review mandate.18 Authors cannot simply link to their public GitHub profiles; any links to external repositories must utilize dedicated anonymization services.19 Furthermore, reviewers are explicitly warned against executing unverified code on their primary workstations due to the inherent security vulnerabilities of untrusted software.19 The organizing committee recommends that reviewers run source code exclusively within network-isolated cloud instances, Docker containers, or Virtual Machine images to prevent exploitation.19 Reviewers are also instructed to exercise extreme caution regarding web links provided in the paper or supplementary materials, as these may covertly log visitor IP addresses, thereby deanonymizing the reviewer and compromising the integrity of the double-blind process.19

## **The Evaluations and Datasets (E\&D) Track: A Philosophical Evolution**

Perhaps the most profound structural shift in the NeurIPS 2026 ecosystem is the conceptual evolution of the former "Datasets and Benchmarks" track into the newly minted "Evaluations and Datasets" (E\&D) Track.20 This nomenclature change is not merely cosmetic; it reflects a maturation in the field's understanding of scientific validity.20 As machine learning models reach unprecedented scales and generalized capabilities, the specific mechanisms utilized to evaluate them shape the ultimate scientific conclusions drawn by the global community.20 In essence, the transition signifies a movement from viewing evaluation as an art to recognizing it as a rigorous science.22

### **The Epistemology of Evaluation as a Scientific Object**

The 2026 E\&D track explicitly defines *evaluation itself* as a first-class scientific object.20 Historically, the track was dominated by submissions that simply introduced larger corpora of data or presented benchmarks where the primary contribution was demonstrating that a novel algorithm could beat a baseline score.21 The new framing explicitly dictates that a submission need not "beat a baseline" to be accepted; its primary scientific contribution should be to deepen, contextualize, and refine our fundamental understanding of evaluation practices.21

The broadened scope actively solicits and embraces a wide array of conceptual and empirical contributions. Authors are encouraged to submit work that deeply analyzes the strengths, hidden limitations, and catastrophic failure modes of existing, widely adopted benchmarks.20 There is a specific call for longitudinal studies examining benchmark saturation and overfitting, exploring how the relentless optimization against static test sets artificially inflates community perceptions of progress and distorts broader scientific conclusions.20 Furthermore, the track welcomes rigorous independent reproductions, extensive auditing, and aggressive stress-testing (often referred to as red-teaming) of prior, highly cited evaluations to verify their ongoing robustness.2

The E\&D track also seeks innovations in qualitative oversight, actively soliciting the development of advanced documentation methodologies.20 This includes the proposal and refinement of structured artifacts like Data Cards, Model Cards, and Evaluation Cards, which are designed to standardize how evaluative claims are historically interpreted and compared across disparate models.2 While classical benchmarking and novel dataset curation remain fully in scope, they are now appropriately contextualized as subsets of the broader evaluative science—standardized, reusable setups that serve as shared comparison points.21 Examples of ideal submissions range from introducing massive, structured databases for specific domain applications (e.g., novel computer vision taxonomies) to deep explorations of the inherent trade-offs in fair risk score determination, or systematic analyses of machine learning challenges and prior NeurIPS competitions that yield critical new insights into algorithmic behavior.20

### **Review Dynamics: The Shift to Double-Blind**

Historically, dataset submissions operated under single-blind constraints due to the inherent logistical difficulty of anonymizing massive, highly unique, or institutionally linked data releases. For the 2026 conference, the default review mechanism for the E\&D track has fundamentally shifted to **double-blind**.2 This aligns the track's intellectual rigor with the Main conference, reflecting the inclusion of conceptual evaluation papers, auditing frameworks, and methodological critiques that require no massive data releases and can be easily anonymized.2

However, the organizing committee maintains a pragmatic flexibility for authors whose primary contribution remains centered on massive data curation. Authors introducing novel, large-scale datasets—where anonymization is computationally intractable or logically impossible due to the dataset's very nature—may explicitly opt into a single-blind review.2 This is achieved typographically by utilizing the specific LaTeX configuration \\usepackage\[eandd, nonanonymous\]{neurips\_2026} during document compilation.23 In boundary cases where an evaluation framework or benchmark builds extensively upon an existing, uniquely identifiable public codebase, authors are nonetheless expected to execute a "best-effort" anonymization protocol to protect the integrity of the double-blind ideal as much as possible.2

Unlike the Main track, where datasets and source code are classified as supplementary materials, the E\&D track designates datasets and executable code as core, mandatory components of the submission itself.23 Consequently, there are no grace periods; the full dataset and the code required to utilize it must be submitted in their final, accessible form by the May 6 full paper deadline.23

To combat the pervasive issue of link rot and ensure the longitudinal reproducibility of accepted research, the 2026 guidelines strictly enforce where and how datasets may be hosted. ## **Machine-Readable Standardization: The Croissant Metadata Framework**

As the sheer volume of machine learning datasets expands exponentially, the primary barrier to scientific reproducibility has shifted from mere data availability to data interoperability and discoverability. To solve this systemic issue, NeurIPS 2026 has strictly mandated the use of the **Croissant** metadata format for all dataset submissions across the E\&D track.2

Developed through a participatory, open design process by the MLCommons working group—comprising stakeholders from academia, industry, and research organizations—Croissant is a high-level, machine-readable format built directly as an extension to the established schema.org vocabulary.25 It operationalizes dataset documentation by structuring complex metadata across four deeply integrated layers 26:

1. **Metadata**: The high-level description of the dataset, including licensing, provenance, and responsible ML aspects.26  
2. **Resources**: The precise localization of one or more files, APIs, or streams containing the raw underlying data.26  
3. **Structure**: The mechanical definition of how the raw data is mathematically combined, normalized, and arranged into usable data structures (e.g., managing relational schemas for tabular data or Parquet conversions).26  
4. **ML Semantics**: The contextual definition of how the data is most frequently utilized within a machine learning context, outlining default feature splits and target variables.26

The adoption of Croissant is critical because it allows datasets to be instantly discoverable across global aggregators like Google Dataset Search, and enables the automatic loading of datasets into popular machine learning toolkits such as PyTorch and JAX without requiring authors to write bespoke dataloading scripts.25

### **The Mandatory RAI (Responsible AI) Extension**

While preferred hosting platforms like Hugging Face, Kaggle, Harvard Dataverse, and OpenML automatically generate the core Croissant fields (such as licensing, author names, and basic structural descriptions) based on the repository's native interface, authors are absolutely required to manually append the **Croissant Responsible AI (RAI) extension** fields prior to submitting their file to OpenReview.2 In previous conference cycles, while general metadata compliance was high, adoption of the RAI extension remained severely minimal; for 2026, it is a strictly enforced submission requirement.22

The RAI extension demands explicit, structured, machine-readable string (sc:Text) or datetime (sc:DateTime) responses detailing the ethical, temporal, and statistical realities of the dataset.29 This forces researchers to deeply interrogate the provenance and potential harms of their data collection processes.

| Croissant RAI Property | Required Schema Type | Evaluative Purpose and Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| rai:personalSensitiveInformation | sc:Text | Cataloging embedded human attributes within the data, including gender, age, geography, language, and socio-economic status.29 |
| rai:dataSocialImpact | sc:Text | Explicating the potential downstream societal consequences of models trained on this specific distribution.29 |
| rai:dataBiases | sc:Text | Identifying systemic skews, underrepresentation, or historical prejudices encoded directly within the raw data.29 |
| rai:dataLimitations | sc:Text | Defining distribution limits, data quality issues, noise ratios, and strictly non-recommended use cases.29 |
| rai:dataCollectionRawData | sc:Text | Verifying the exact source and provenance of the raw data (e.g., specific web domains scraped, sensors utilized).29 |
| rai:dataCollectionTimeframe | sc:DateTime | Establishing the strict temporal bounds (start and end dates) of the data scraping or collection process.29 |
| rai:dataImputationProtocol | sc:Text | Documenting the specific algorithmic techniques used to estimate, interpolate, or fill missing values within the set.29 |
| rai:dataManipulationProtocol | sc:Text | Detailing any normalization, pruning, filtering, or structural alterations applied to the raw source data.29 |

Authors must validate their manually enriched .jsonld Croissant files using the official online Croissant validator tool—frequently hosted via Hugging Face spaces—before submitting the final confirmation to OpenReview.2 The integration of these fields ensures that the ethical guardrails, documented limitations, and structural biases of a dataset travel symbiotically with the raw tensors wherever the data is subsequently downloaded and deployed.28

## **The Position Papers Track: Defending the Future of the Field**

Reintroduced for its second highly anticipated year, the Position Papers Track serves a uniquely critical function within the NeurIPS ecosystem. Unlike the Main or E\&D tracks, which strictly report on empirical advances, theoretical proofs, and data curations that *have already been accomplished*, the Position track provides a rigorous, high-visibility forum to argue for *what should be done* moving forward.9 It is an arena for epistemological debate, profound methodological critique, and the strategic redirection of community resources toward neglected or emerging paradigms.9

### **Strategic Alignment, Timeline Synchronization, and Scope Broadening**

In its inaugural experimental year, the Position track suffered from temporal misalignment; the lag in the review and decision timeline relative to the other tracks caused authors to miss subsequent submission deadlines for alternative venues, leading to community frustration.6 For 2026, the track's timeline has been perfectly synchronized with the Main track, operating on the exact same May 6 full paper submission and September 24 notification schedule.6

Furthermore, the organizing committee has explicitly broadened the scope to counter a perceived bias against deeply technical arguments that emerged during the previous cycle.6 The track actively seeks highly technical, mathematical, or architectural critiques, provided they are framed as systemic arguments about the field's trajectory rather than mere localized algorithmic improvements.6 A paper whose primary claim reduces to "our novel optimization method is superior, and the community should immediately adopt it" will be desk-rejected from the Position track and redirected to the Main program.6

### **Evaluation Criteria: Novelty, Rigour, and Significance**

Submissions to the Position track are evaluated against three highly specific pillars, distinct from the heuristics used in the Main track 9:

1. **Novelty and Significance**: The manuscript must address a debate of profound contemporary urgency. Authors must convince the multidisciplinary target community that the topic is relevant and expose deeper perspectives that challenge prevailing orthodoxy, creating opportunities for discourse that would not otherwise find a home in standard proceedings.9  
2. **Rigour in Argumentation**: Claims must be grounded in objective reality and methodological soundness. The urgency of a societal threat or the perceived importance of a technical bottleneck does not excuse sloppy, purely speculative reasoning.9 Arguments may be supported through a wide array of methods—including formal mathematical reasoning, empirical literature synthesis, cross-disciplinary analysis, or logical deduction—but must apply consistently high methodological standards regardless of the chosen paradigm.9  
3. **Refutation Architecture**: The track fundamentally champions intellectual pluralism. Authors are heavily encouraged—and implicitly expected—to include dedicated sections directly within the main body text (such as an "Alternative Views" or "Objections and Counterarguments" section) to preemptively address, respect, and logically dismantle the strongest counterarguments to their position.9

### **Distinctive Formatting and Submission Logistics**

Position papers adhere to the standard 9-page limit and double-blind formatting rules, but diverge sharply in several highly specific areas to reinforce the unique nature of the track 9:

* **The Position Title**: The title of the paper must explicitly and aggressively state the position being defended. Passive or purely descriptive titles are discouraged. Appropriate examples provided by the committee include assertive declarations such as "Quantum Atelic Learning Methods Should Employ Psychic Insights" or "Stop Research on Psychic Properties of Machine Learning".9  
* **The Bold Introduction**: The central thesis of the manuscript must be summarizable in under three sentences, and this exact summary must be printed in **bold text** explicitly within the introduction section of the paper.9 Furthermore, the abstract must begin with a clear declaration, such as "This position paper argues that...".9  
* **The Rationale Requirement**: At the OpenReview submission stage, authors are required to submit a short paragraph (maximum 250 words) defending exactly why their manuscript is uniquely suited for the Position track rather than the Main track. This text assists meta-reviewers during triage but is excluded from the final compiled PDF.9  
* **AI Tool Disclosures**: The intellectual substance and primary drafting of position papers must be fundamentally human. The use of Large Language Models (LLMs) is strictly confined to peripheral tasks, such as copy-editing, translation assistance, and basic grammar correction. Any utilization of AI assistance must be explicitly disclosed at the time of submission.9  
* **Exclusion of the Checklist**: Because position papers rarely introduce new datasets, codebases, or massive computational experiments, the standard NeurIPS Paper Checklist is uniquely waived for this specific track.9 To assist in reviewer allocation, authors must instead indicate the prevailing disciplines or methodologies utilized in their argumentation during the submission process.9

## **Research Ethics, Consent, and Responsible AI**

The NeurIPS Code of Ethics serves as the philosophical and legal bedrock for the conference, guiding the global community toward higher standards of societal impact mitigation, environmental awareness, and research integrity.3 The Code of Ethics complements the standard Code of Conduct (which focuses on plagiarism, fraud, and professional behavior) by targeting the systemic potential for harm inherent in the research process itself.3 Reviewers are empowered—and explicitly instructed—to flag submissions for an independent Ethics Review if a manuscript demonstrates a reckless disregard for data provenance, user privacy, or the potential for downstream societal harm.12 These flagged papers are assigned dedicated ethics reviewers who effectively join the program committee to adjudicate the severity of the violations.12

### **Data Sourcing, Consent, and Privacy Protocols**

The era of indiscriminate web scraping without legal or ethical consequence has definitively ended. The 2026 guidelines dictate stringent requirements for any research interacting with human-centric datasets 3:

* **Explicit Human Consent**: Any paper that chooses to create a novel dataset utilizing real data from real human subjects must either secure explicit, informed consent from those participants, or provide an exhaustive, legally sound explanation as to why securing consent was fundamentally impossible or ill-advised.3  
* **Privacy Minimization and Sanitization**: Datasets must undergo aggressive sanitization to minimize the exposure of Personally Identifiable Information (PII). Authors are mandated to leverage modern privacy protocols, cryptographic encryption, and sophisticated anonymization techniques to safeguard against data leakage, theft, and re-identification attacks.3  
* **Fair Wages and Crowdsourcing**: If the research relies on human alignment, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), or general data labeling via crowdsourcing platforms or contract work, authors must guarantee and document that workers received fair compensation that respects minimum hourly wage standards relative to their geography.3  
* **Copyright and Deprecated Datasets**: While the norms of fair use in machine learning remain legally fluid, authors must respect the explicit terms of service and defined licenses (e.g., MIT, CC-BY 4.0) of any data sources scraped.3 Furthermore, authors hold the burden of verifying with original creators that historical datasets utilized as baselines have not been officially deprecated, retracted, or removed due to ethical violations.3 Claims of diverse or universal representation within a dataset must be substantiated by concrete demographic evidence.3

### **Societal Impact Mitigation and Dual-Use Risks**

Beyond the mechanics of data collection, the algorithmic research itself must be interrogated for its potential to inflict harm upon deployment. Contributors must prospectively evaluate whether their technology can be utilized to injure, deceive, or exploit people through direct application, side effects, or intentional misuse.3

Researchers must specifically assess security risks, investigating whether their applications could open critical vulnerabilities or cause severe accidents when deployed in uncontrolled, real-world environments.3 This is particularly relevant for synthetic media generators (deepfakes) and autonomous agents.3 Crucially, the transparent documentation of these "dual-use" risks is not viewed as a weakness. Rather than acting as a rationale for rejection, the honest documentation of potential societal harm, accompanied by proposed mitigation strategies and responsible licensing models, demonstrates scientific maturity and is actively rewarded during the ethics review phase.3

## **Formatting, Accessibility, and the Presentation of Data**

The rigid formatting requirements enforced by NeurIPS are not merely stylistic preferences; they are deliberately designed to guarantee algorithmic legibility for archival processing systems and, critically, universal accessibility for visually impaired researchers utilizing assistive technologies.30 NeurIPS closely aligns its formatting standards with broader web accessibility guidelines (such as WCAG 22\) to ensure that the scientific discourse is available to all practitioners regardless of physical disability.31

Authors must implement a series of specific accessibility standardizations during the generation of their final PDFs, ensuring that the visual representation of their data is translatable into accessible formats 30:

1. **Marked-Up Mathematical Equations**: Mathematical formulas must never be embedded into the PDF as static images (e.g., PNG/JPEG screenshots). They must be rendered via native LaTeX markup so that screen reading software can parse the complex mathematical syntax audibly and accurately.30  
2. **Structural Native Tables**: Empirical results, hyperparameters, and structured comparisons must be presented using native LaTeX tables (e.g., utilizing the tabular environment). Authors must explicitly define which cells function as headers and maintain logical, simple column structures without excessive cell merging.30 Presenting complex numerical data as an image of a spreadsheet grid is strictly prohibited, as it entirely destroys the ability of screen readers to navigate the relational data.30  
3. **Alternative Text and Descriptive Captions**: All figures, graphs, complex plots, and non-text media must include comprehensive descriptive Alt-Text.30  
4. **Color Independence and Legibility**: While the use of color figures is permitted and encouraged for complex multidimensional plots, the core scientific information must not rely *solely* on color differentiation for comprehension.13 Authors must ensure sufficient contrast ratios between text and background elements.32 Furthermore, plots should utilize varying line styles (dashes, dots) or distinct markers so that the data remains legible, unambiguous, and scientifically valid whether the paper is printed in grayscale or viewed by individuals with color vision deficiencies.13 The use of spatial ASCII art for diagrams is generally discouraged unless it is inherently parseable by standard assistive technologies.31

## **Reproducibility and the NeurIPS Paper Checklist**

To systematically enforce transparency, accountability, and methodological hygiene, all submissions to the Main and E\&D tracks must incorporate the 15-question NeurIPS Paper Checklist.5 Integrated directly into the LaTeX style file, this checklist acts as a codified forcing function, requiring authors to explicitly answer "Yes," "No," or "N/A" regarding various facets of their research.17 The answers are visible to reviewers, area chairs, and ethics reviewers, forming an integral part of the evaluation matrix.17

The checklist interrogates claims of theoretical generalizability, the rigorous handling of error bars (requiring explicit documentation within the text of how confidence intervals were mathematically calculated), and the comprehensive detailing of computational resource consumption.17 When documenting compute, authors must detail the specific hardware utilized (e.g., internal GPU clusters versus specific cloud providers), the memory constraints, and the total execution time required to reproduce the exact experiments detailed in the paper.17 Furthermore, authors are asked to disclose whether the full research project required vastly more compute than the final experiments reported, providing a transparent view into the true cost of the research (including preliminary or failed experiments).17

Crucially, an answer of "No" on the checklist is not immediate grounds for desk rejection, provided the authors supply a rigorous, intellectually honest justification for the omission.17 The checklist is designed to illuminate limitations, not to punish researchers for operating under constraints. If aspirational goals are utilized to motivate the research direction in the introduction, the authors must clearly delineate where the current paper falls short of those ultimate objectives to prevent exaggerated claims.17

The burden of accurately completing this checklist has led to extensive experimentation. During the NeurIPS 2024 cycle, the conference introduced an optional Large Language Model (LLM) "Checklist Assistant" (utilizing OpenAI's GPT-4) to provide pre-submission feedback to authors regarding checklist compliance.33 Out of 17,491 total conference submissions, 539 authors completed pre-usage surveys, and the system evaluated responses against the full paper text (including appendices) using prompt engineering.33 Post-usage surveys indicated that 46 out of 65 distinct respondents found the tool useful and used the LLM's feedback to actively revise their papers or checklist responses prior to the deadline, highlighting the increasing intersection of AI in the administrative governance of AI research.33

## **Reviewer Dynamics, Rebuttal Procedures, and Final Decisions**

Following the May 6 full paper deadline, the evaluation framework shifts to the global reviewing cohort. Reviewers are instructed to evaluate the submissions along a 6-point numeric scale, providing granular, highly constructive feedback on the paper's overarching strengths, specific weaknesses, and ethical implications.12 The scale operates with specific thresholds: a score of "6: Weak Accept" designates a technically solid manuscript with moderate-to-high impact and no severe ethical or reproducibility concerns; a "5: Borderline Accept" indicates a novel concept where the reasons to accept outweigh the reasons to reject, despite limitations such as a restricted empirical evaluation; and a "4: Borderline Reject" signifies a technically sound paper hampered by severe limitations.12

A critical and highly dynamic phase of the NeurIPS lifecycle is the Author Rebuttal and Discussion period, scheduled for late July and early August 2026\.12 During this window, authors have the opportunity to engage in a rolling, direct dialogue with their assigned reviewers via the OpenReview interface.19 This is not merely an administrative formality; reviewers are explicitly mandated to participate deeply in this discourse, particularly for borderline papers or submissions where reviewer assessments wildly diverge.19 If an author successfully clarifies a theoretical ambiguity, provides missing context, or addresses an empirical deficit via the rebuttal, reviewers are expected to actively revise their initial scores and update their written reviews to reflect the new consensus.19

The integrity of this process is paramount. Reviewers who submit late reviews or provide feedback of insufficient quality may face penalties, including losing access to the reviews on their own co-authored submissions, as part of the conference's responsible reviewing initiatives.19 Furthermore, the organizing committee actively measures the quality and effectiveness of the review process via randomized controlled experiments, approved by an Institutional Review Board (IRB), to continuously refine the scientific evaluation pipeline year over year.5 Following the conclusion of the discussion period and subsequent SAC-AC deliberations, the final acceptance decisions are broadcast to the global community on September 24, 2026\.12

The NeurIPS 2026 submission framework represents a highly complex, interrelated system of scientific governance. From the strict adherence to universally synchronized OpenReview timelines and the philosophical broadening of the Evaluations and Datasets Track, to the epistemological debates fostered by the Position Papers Track and the deep machine-readability enforced by the Croissant metadata standard, the conference demands holistic excellence. By mastering these intricate formatting constraints, understanding the philosophical directives of the specific tracks, and integrating rigorous, ethical metadata documentation, researchers can ensure their contributions successfully navigate the exhaustive peer-review process and actively shape the future trajectory of global artificial intelligence.
